Antiarrhythmic Drugs MCQs

Case-Based Questions

Q1. Case: A 65-year-old male with atrial fibrillation...

A 65-year-old male presents with atrial fibrillation and a heart rate of 140 bpm. He has a history of hypertension but no heart failure.

Which drug is most appropriate to control his heart rate?

A) Lidocaine

B) Amiodarone

C) Metoprolol

D) Quinidine

Answer: C) Metoprolol

Explanation: Metoprolol, a Class II antiarrhythmic (beta-blocker), is ideal for rate control in atrial fibrillation, especially in a patient with hypertension and no heart failure. Lidocaine (Class IB) is for ventricular arrhythmias, amiodarone (Class III) is broader but has more side effects, and quinidine (Class IA) is less preferred due to proarrhythmic risks.

Q2. Case: A 50-year-old female with ventricular tachycardia...

A 50-year-old female with ventricular tachycardia post-myocardial infarction is hemodynamically stable.

What is the first-line antiarrhythmic drug?

A) Procainamide

B) Lidocaine

C) Sotalol

D) Flecainide

Answer: B) Lidocaine

Explanation: Lidocaine (Class IB) is the first-line drug for ventricular tachycardia in the acute post-MI setting due to its efficacy and safety in ischemic tissue. Procainamide (Class IA) and flecainide (Class IC) are less preferred, and sotalol (Class III) is more for maintenance.

Q3. Case: A 30-year-old male with WPW syndrome...

A 30-year-old male with Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) syndrome presents with atrial fibrillation and a rapid ventricular response.

Which drug should be avoided?

A) Procainamide

B) Digoxin

C) Amiodarone

D) Ibutilide

Answer: B) Digoxin

Explanation: Digoxin is contraindicated in WPW with atrial fibrillation as it can enhance accessory pathway conduction, worsening the arrhythmia. Procainamide, amiodarone, or ibutilide are safer options to slow conduction or convert the rhythm.

Q4. Case: A 70-year-old man with chronic atrial fibrillation...

A 70-year-old man with chronic atrial fibrillation develops hypotension after starting an antiarrhythmic drug. His ECG shows a widened QRS.

Which drug is most likely responsible?

A) Flecainide

B) Atenolol

C) Diltiazem

D) Verapamil

Answer: A) Flecainide

Explanation: Flecainide (Class IC) can cause QRS widening and hypotension due to its sodium channel blockade, especially in overdose or structural heart disease. Beta-blockers (atenolol) and calcium channel blockers (diltiazem, verapamil) typically don’t widen QRS.

Q5. Case: A patient with paroxysmal SVT...

A patient with paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardia (PSVT) is treated with an IV drug that terminates the episode within seconds.

Which drug was likely used?

A) Adenosine

B) Amiodarone

C) Lidocaine

D) Sotalol

Answer: A) Adenosine

Explanation: Adenosine is the drug of choice for acute termination of PSVT due to its rapid onset and short half-life, acting on AV node conduction. The others are not first-line for PSVT.

Q7. Case: A patient with ventricular fibrillation...

Which drug should be administered next?

Q8. Case: A 40-year-old with atrial flutter...

Which drug should be avoided in heart failure?

Answer: A) Flecainide

Explanation: Flecainide (Class IC) has negative inotropic effects and is contraindicated in heart failure. Amiodarone and dofetilide (Class III) are safer options in this population.

High-Yield Questions

Q26. Which class of antiarrhythmics blocks sodium channels...

Which class of antiarrhythmics blocks sodium channels and prolongs the action potential?

A) Class IA

B) Class IB

C) Class IC

D) Class III

Answer: A) Class IA

Explanation: Class IA (e.g., quinidine, procainamide) blocks sodium channels and prolongs action potential duration, unlike IB (shortens) and IC (no effect on duration).

Q27. Which drug is a pure potassium channel blocker...

Which drug is a pure potassium channel blocker?

A) Amiodarone

B) Sotalol

C) Dofetilide

D) Ibutilide

Answer: C) Dofetilide

Explanation: Dofetilide is a pure Class III agent, blocking potassium channels only, unlike amiodarone (multi-class) or sotalol (beta-blocker + Class III).

Q28. Which antiarrhythmic has the shortest half-life...

Which antiarrhythmic has the shortest half-life?

A) Adenosine

B) Amiodarone

C) Lidocaine

D) Sotalol

Answer: A) Adenosine

Explanation: Adenosine has a half-life of seconds, making it ideal for acute SVT termination, compared to hours or days for the others.

Q29. Which class is contraindicated in structural heart disease...

Which class is contraindicated in structural heart disease?

A) Class IA

B) Class IC

C) Class III

D) Class IV

Answer: B) Class IC

Explanation: Class IC (e.g., flecainide, propafenone) increases mortality in structural heart disease (e.g., post-MI) due to proarrhythmic effects (CAST trial).

Q30. Which drug has both beta-blocker and potassium channel-blocking effects...

Which drug has both beta-blocker and potassium channel-blocking effects?

A) Sotalol

B) Propranolol

C) Amiodarone

D) Metoprolol

Answer: A) Sotalol

Explanation: Sotalol is unique with Class II (beta-blockade) and Class III (potassium channel blockade) effects, unlike pure beta-blockers or amiodarone.

Q31. Which antiarrhythmic is most likely to cause torsades de pointes...

Which antiarrhythmic is most likely to cause torsades de pointes?

A) Quinidine

B) Lidocaine

C) Flecainide

D) Verapamil

Answer: A) Quinidine

Explanation: Quinidine (Class IA) prolongs QT and is a classic cause of torsades, unlike lidocaine (no QT effect) or flecainide (QRS widening).

Q32. Which drug is used for digitalis-induced arrhythmias...

Which drug is used for digitalis-induced arrhythmias?

Which class slows AV node conduction?

D) Class III

Answer: C) Both A and B

Explanation: Class II (beta-blockers) and Class IV (calcium channel blockers) both slow AV node conduction, useful in SVT and AF rate control.
